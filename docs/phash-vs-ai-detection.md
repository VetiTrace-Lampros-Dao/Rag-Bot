# Perceptual Hashing vs. AI Detection

A common misconception about VeriTrace is that its perceptual hashing system can detect whether content was generated by artificial intelligence. This is incorrect, and it is important to understand the distinction clearly to set accurate expectations.

## What Perceptual Hashing Actually Does

Perceptual hashing detects visual similarity between two pieces of content. It answers one specific question: "Does this image, video, or document look like something already registered in VeriTrace?" It does this by comparing the structural fingerprint of the submitted content against fingerprints stored in the system's index. The output is a similarity score and, if a match is found, the record of the original registration.

Perceptual hashing does not answer the question: "Was this content generated by DALL-E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, or any other AI tool versus captured with a physical camera?" The pHash algorithm is entirely media-agnostic in this regard. It treats AI-generated images and camera-captured photographs identically. A photograph and an AI-generated image that happen to look visually similar will produce similar perceptual hashes. Two completely different AI-generated images will produce different perceptual hashes. The origin of the content is invisible to the hashing algorithm.

## The AI Tool Attribution Field

VeriTrace does record an `ai_tool` attribution field in its content registry. This field can contain values like "DALL-E 3", "Midjourney", "Stable Diffusion", or be left empty for non-AI content. However, this is a self-reported label provided by the creator at registration time. The system does not detect, infer, or verify this label in any way. A creator could register AI-generated content without declaring an AI tool, or declare an AI tool for content that was not AI-generated. VeriTrace records the declaration as given.

## Honest Answer for Users

If a user asks whether VeriTrace can detect AI-generated images, the honest and accurate answer is: VeriTrace can tell you whether a specific image, or something visually similar to it, was previously registered in the system and by whom. It can also tell you what AI tool label, if any, the original registrant declared. But it cannot determine how the image was originally created. Its sole purpose is provenance tracking and near-duplicate detection, not content origin classification.
